Situational Awareness

Situational Awareness has a dual meaning. One that is in regards to the individual and the other dealing with groups acting as a team. With regard to the individual; I cannot stress more. One must be very diligent and aware. Not just of what is going on around you but why things are going on around you. Sure you need that thousand yard stare, but it's more than that.

By definition Situational Awareness is the ability to identify, process, and comprehend information about your surroundings in order to identify and react to CAPD. It is dynamic, hard to maintain, and very easy to lose. Knowing what is going on all the time is difficult for any one person at best, especially during high stress or survival situations. To establishing a proper mindset is first to recognize that threats exist. Denial or ignorance of a threat makes your chances of quickly recognizing an emerging threat and avoiding it highly problematic and unlikely. Bad things can and do happen, especially in survival situations. Apathy, denial and complacency often prove to be very deadly. Learning to trust your "gut", your intuition is key. Many times a your subconscious can notice subtle signs of danger that the conscious mind has difficulty quantifying. Trusting your initial gut feelings about a situation and avoiding the resulting CAPD can be very inconvenient. It can even cause you to lose out on opportunities, but ignoring those feelings can lead you to potential disaster. Be pragmatic. Is the CAPD worth the gains.

The hard part is discipline; practicing situational awareness means you'll have to put forth the conscious effort required to pay attention to your gut feelings and to the events surrounding you daily life even while you are busy and distracted. It is at such times that even obvious hostile activity can go unnoticed. You need to learn to be observant even while doing other things.

Situational Awareness as it relates to a group effort deals mainly with traveling as a group or patrolling. Both will be discussed at length later on as part of the training section of this column.
